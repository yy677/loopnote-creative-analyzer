How the scoring works

LF (lower-funnel, conversion efficiency) combines three min-max normalized signals: cost per paid start (50%, inverted so lower is better), paid starts volume (30%), trial starts volume (20%). CPA gets the highest weight because the campaign brief names CPA as the primary goal.

MF (mid-funnel, engagement quality) combines CTR (40%, closest to intent), 6s hold rate (35%, closest to creative quality), and thumbstop rate (25%, lowest weight because it's most prone to being clickbait-driven).

UF (upper-funnel, awareness) would normally include impressions, reach, brand search lift. Not in this dataset, so omitted from scoring (acknowledged, not faked).

Overall = LF × weight + MF × (1 − weight). Default 70/30 because the brief optimizes for paid starts, but the weight is adjustable above for different campaign goals.

Status thresholds: Winning ≥ 75, Average 55–74, Inefficient < 55. These are heuristics, not hard cutoffs — use the score, not the label, when deciding scale-or-kill.

7. Methodology and design choices

Why funnel-weighted scoring instead of a single CPA ranking?

A pure CPA sort would penalize creatives with strong engagement that simply haven't been scaled yet, and over-reward small-volume winners. Splitting LF (efficiency) and MF (engagement) lets the team separate "this creative converts" from "this creative resonates" — useful when triaging fatigue vs. funnel issues.

Why min-max normalization?

The 10 creatives span vastly different scales (CPA from $62 to $333). Z-scores would be sensitive to the small sample and outliers. Min-max keeps every creative on a 0–1 comparable scale and is robust enough for a weekly creative review where the goal is ranking, not statistical inference.

Why surface counter-signals?

Growth teams routinely scale the "highest CTR" or "highest thumbstop" creative and lose money. The counter-signals section explicitly checks for these traps — high engagement with bad downstream conversion, or low-spend creatives prematurely tagged as failures.

What this tool does not do (yet)

No statistical significance testing (sample sizes here don't support it). No incrementality modeling. No creative-fatigue curve. No competitor data ingestion. These are the obvious v2 directions if this gets used in production.
